# General
Lecture

The lecture will cover basic and advanced topics on computer animation and (real-time) simulation.

Animation topics will be largely based on a book.

The theoretical knowledge will be evaluated based on a final written exam.

Exercises

In programming exercises, students will implement algorithms and techniques from the lecture in a JavaScript framework based on THREE.js. In an additional motion capture exercise, students will be able to use the Mixed Reality Lab of TU Wien to record a motion capture sequence of themselves and animate a 3d character using the recorded sequence. For more details, see Exercises.
In short, to get the maximum points for the exercises, the following requirements need to be fulfilled:

    Your submission must work/look like specified in the instructions. 
    You need to be able to understand and explain your code in the exercise presentation.


For late submissions, we deduct 10% of the achieved points for each day.

Project

The second part of the exercise involves a group project (2-person groups) where you can choose to either:

(1) Produce a short 1-3-minute animation film

OR

(2) Implement an interactive simulation of your own choosing

For more details, see Project

## Exercises

There will be five exercises. For each exercise, you will have at least 1 week to complete it. For exercises 1-4, you are required to work on your own. Exercise 5 will be done in the same group of 2 as the final project.

Each exercise is worth 8 points of your final grade, for a total maximum of 40 points for the exercise.
Exercise Grading

For exercises 1-4, you will be required to indicate in your submission which tasks you have solved, which will form the basis of your grade for each exercise. In the Exercise Presentation, you will then be asked to present and explain your implementation of at least one of these tasks. The functionality and explanation will then determine your grade for the exercise. If you fail to explain the implementation, you will not receive any points for the exercise.

Exercise 5 will be graded based on the final video containing your motion capture sequence.

## Project
For the final project, which is done in groups of two, there are two options from which each group has to choose one at the beginning of the project phase. A group sign-up will be available here on TUWEL before the group phase starts. You may contact your colleagues during the lecture or use the Q&A Forum to find your group partner.
Option 1: Animated Short Film

If you choose this option, you will create an animated short film lasting 1-3 minutes that showcases the animation and simulation techniques presented in the lecture. For this film, you do not have to implement the effects yourself; you may instead use animation software (Blender, Maya, ...) or a game engine (Unity, Unreal Engine, ...). The final submission for this project will be the rendered video of your short film.

For some inspiration, see the example submissions from a previous year below (note, however, that in the previous version, the short film was not a group project). In addition to implementing techniques from the lecture in general, you are also encouraged to incorporate your motion capture animated character into the short film. Therefore, it may make sense to already have an idea of your story prior to the motion capture recording session.

The following deliverables are expected for this project option:
Proposal phase:

    Story, consisting of
        Plot (summary of the story in one sentence)
        Narration (a 100-300 word description of the story)
    Storyboard
        One drawing for each scene of the film
    Presentation, containing:
        The above-mentioned elements
        The intended animation techniques to be used
        The intended animation pipeline/software tools to be used


Final submission:

    The final rendered video
    Presentation, containing:
        The used animation techniques
        Challenges and learnings
        Description of the creation process (i.e., what assets/plugins/etc were used, which parts are self-made, ...)

Option 2: Simulation Project

For some inspiration, see the links with further resources on simulations below. You may also conduct your own literature review to identify interesting papers on simulation techniques and scenarios.

The following deliverables are expected for this project option:
Proposal Phase:

    Short 1-2 page max proposal containing:
        The scenario to be simulated
        Theoretical background (e.g., model describing the process, a research paper, ...)
        The planned implementation approach
        Some literature/related work on the proposed simulation scenario
    Presentation summarizing the contents of the proposal


Final submission:

    Video/live showcase of simulation 
    Presentation containing:
        Used algorithms/heuristics
        Implementation details
        Challenges/learnings 


Project Grading

For the project, you can reach a total of 30 points made up of 5 for the proposal presentation, 20 for the implementation, and 5 for the final presentation. A detailed grading rubric used to determine the final point score can be found here.

## FAQ

To whom should I address my request?

This depends on the request. First, read this FAQ. Maybe this already answers your question.

If the question could be interesting also for other people, please post it into our Q&A forum here in TUWEL.

If you decide to write an e-mail:

    If you have a question about the exercises or the project (including all submissions and project presentations), you need to include the people organizing the practical part of the VU (see contacts). 
    If you have a question about the lecture exam or lecture, you need to include the course and lecture organizers (see contacts). 

Important notes:

    Since we receive many e-mails, it might be that you do not receive an answer if you address the wrong person(s)! Make sure you pick the correct recipients. 
    We may need some days to answer e-mails. During holidays, it may even take longer. 

Do I need prior knowledge for this course?

Basic knowledge in computer graphics is useful, but not required. In the first few lecture units, we will repeat the fundamentals such as transformations.

Programming experience in JavaScript is required and will not be taught in this course. We provide some materials for learning JavaScript and THREE.js, though.

I do not need the exam, I just want to listen in. Is this possible?

Yes. As long as you do not take the exam, you will not be graded. Everybody is welcome to join the lectures!

I don't want to participate in the course any more. How can I drop out?

This depends on what you have already done. If you have only registered for TISS / TUWEL, you can just stop attending the course without any consequences. If you have already submitted something, you will receive a grade at the end of the semester taking into account all your contributions for the course. Please have a look at the course information if your contributions will be sufficient for a positive grade.

I don't want to participate in the course any more. Please deregister me from TUWEL.

We do not deregister students from TUWEL. If you do not want to receive any notifications any more, go into the forums and unsubscribe manually. You can also manually remove the course from your TUWEL dashboard. You will not receive any grade if you stay in the TUWEL course (unless you have already submitted something).

Can I use AI to solve the exercises?

No. The goal of the practical exercises to make your own hands-on experiences. AI usage is therefore generally not allowed and considered plagiarism. In the lab, students will have to answer questions about their code. Answering these questions is no problem if you have produced the code on your own. However, if the code has not been produced by yourself (because you copied it from somewhere or because you used an AI to generate it), answering questions will be very difficult. Students who cannot answer questions about their code do not get any points for their exercise submissions!

Can I use AI for my project?

Yes, but... For the project, it is allowed to use auxiliary libraries and AI support. However, AI-generated code has to be clearly documented (see TU guidelines). In fact, it will be interesting to learn about your experiences using AI in the data analysis process at your project presentation! For the project grading, we try to consider the overall effort that goes into the project. AI usage can drastically decrease the time investment, which might be used for other improvements of your project. If we discover undocumented AI usage, the project will be considered plagiarized.

I cannot participate at the project/exercise presentation(s). How can I proceed?

By default, presentations are in presence. If you really cannot come (in person), contact the people responsible for the lab, and we will try to find a solution. The sooner you let us know, the easier it will be to find a solution. If you do not show up without letting us know, you will receive no points for your presentation.

Is there a schedule for the presentations? I want to come just before my presentation and then leave immediately.

Even though we might publish a schedule, the timing is only approximate based on the tentative order of the presentations. By default, presentations are in presence - and that includes your presentation, as well as those of the others. Of course, we want you to also listen to what your colleagues will do, learn from their approaches, and give you the opportunity to give feedback for them. It would be very impolite to only come for your own presentation. If you are not present yet for your own presentation, we will skip your presentation and you will get no points. If you know in advance that you cannot participate the whole time, see above.

There are no video recordings for all of the lectures.

We provide video recordings as a service, but we cannot guarantee that we can provide recordings every time simply due to potential technical problems or the lecturers forgetting to start the recording. If you want to be sure not to miss anything, please attend the lectures in person.

Is it sufficient to just have a look at the slides for the exam?

No. Especially if you do not have prior knowledge, just learning from the slides will not be sufficient. We try to provide video recordings of the lectures in addition, but we do not guarantee them (see above).
In addition, the content of the mandatory readings will be covered in the exam.

I have a question about a lecture content.

Please ask content questions in the Discussion forum. There, everybody has the chance to help you, and the question may also be interesting for other people. Note that we do not have the capacities to answer outside office hours, and that we generally may respond with delay. Thus, last-minute questions may not receive a response on time.

How will the exam look like?

See the exam information here.

When do I need to do the exam and what happens if I fail the exam?

We offer an exam date in June and one in September. Students doing the exam in June will receive their certificate before the end of July. All other students will receive a certificate at the end of September / in October.

What happens if I fail the practical part?

See grading information here. Since the practical part is not repeatable, the course has to be repeated in the upcoming year in case of a negative practical part.

I loved this course, is there more like this?

Yes! The research unit of computer graphics offers a variety of courses, check out our website for more information.

Also, have a look at our open student topics for project and theses topics!
